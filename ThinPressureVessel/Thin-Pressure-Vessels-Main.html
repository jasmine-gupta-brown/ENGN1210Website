Thin Wall Pressurized Cylinders and Spheres

Consider an internal pressure \(P_i\) which is greater than the outer pressure \(P_o\) acting on a thin-walled hollow structure with thickness \(t << R \; \text{(radius)}\). Let us represent the difference between internal pressure (\(P_i\)) and outer pressure (\(P_o\)) as net pressure \(P\) so \(P = P_i - P_o\) (In the case when outer/external pressure is zero, then \(P=P_i\)).

  1. Thin Wall Cylinder
    Consider a cylindrical coordinate system with position coordinates \((r,\theta, z)\) and coordinate system basis \(\{\bm{\hat{e}}_r, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\theta}, \bm{\hat{e}}_z \}\) for a hollow cylinder that experiences net internal pressure, then \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{\theta\theta} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{t}\\ \sigma_{rr} &= 0 \qquad \qquad \qquad \quad (\textrm{note: } \sigma_{rr} \sim P << \sigma_{\theta\theta} \implies \sigma_{rr} \approx 0), \quad \textrm{and} \end{aligned}\] \[\begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \sigma_{zz} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{2 t} \qquad \qquad \text{(if the axial ends are closed/capped) \quad OR}\\ \sigma_{zz} &= 0 \qquad \qquad \quad \;\; \text{(if the axial ends are open) } \end{cases} \end{aligned}\]

    The material volume approximation for a thin-walled hollow cylinder with length \(L\) is \(V = 2 \pi R \, t \, L\).
    {Note: Volume of material \(V = \pi (R_o^2 - R_i^2) \,L = \pi (R_o+R_i)(R_o-R_i)\, L. \\ R_o - R_i = t\), and for a thin wall case, \(R_o+R_i \approx 2R \implies V \approx 2 \pi R \, t \, L\)}

  2. Thin Wall Sphere
    Consider a spherical coordinate system with position coordinates \((r,\theta, \phi)\) and coordinate system basis \(\{\bm{\hat{e}}_r, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\theta}, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\phi} \}\) for a hollow sphere that experiences net internal pressure, then \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{\theta\theta} = \sigma_{\phi\phi} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{2 t} \\ \sigma_{rr} &= 0 \qquad \qquad (\textrm{note: } \sigma_{rr} \sim P << \sigma_{\theta\theta} \implies \sigma_{rr} \approx 0) \end{aligned}\]

    The material volume approximation for a thin-walled sphere is \(V = 4 \pi R^2 \, t\).
    {Note: Volume of material \(V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (R_o^3 - R_i^3) = \frac{4}{3} \pi (R_o^2+R_i^2+R_oR_i)(R_o-R_i)\)
    with \(R_o - R_i = t\), and for a thin wall case, \(R_o \approx R_i \approx R\), leads to \(V \approx 4 \pi R^2 \, t\)}
