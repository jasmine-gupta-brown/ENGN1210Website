Fracture

Applied stress intensity factor (Mode I): \[K = \sigma^\infty Q \sqrt{\pi a}\] where \(\sigma^\infty\) is the far field stress (external stress), \(Q\) is the geometry and load factor, \(a\) is the characteristic crack length.
The criterion for fracture initiation or crack extension to not occur in response to slowly applied loading of monotonically increasing magnitude is taken as \(K < K_C\), where \(K_C\) is the material and thickness dependent critical value of K and is called the fracture toughness.
The brittle fracture happens when: \(K \ge K_C\)
(Note: Plane strain fracture toughness \({K_I}_c\) is a material property. It is a conservative method of defining fracture toughness, and \({K_I}_c\) is independent of thickness.)

Fatigue

  1. S-N Curve Method:
    The fatigue failure criteria for cyclic loading from multiple stress levels using Miner’s Rule is \[\sum_i \frac{N_i}{{N_f}_i} = 1\] Where \(N_i\) is the number of cycles that have already been run for the i\(^{th}\) stress magnitude, and \({N_f}_i\) is the number of cycles to failure corresponding to the i\(^{th}\) stress amplitude.

  2. Paris’ Law: \[\frac{da}{dN} = C\triangle K^m\]
    Integration of the Paris equation gives: \[\begin{aligned} N_f &= \frac{1}{C\,(\triangle \sigma \,Q \sqrt{\pi})^m} \, \frac{1}{(1-\frac{m}{2})}\, \Big\{a_f^{1-\frac{m}{2}} - a_i^{1-\frac{m}{2}}\Big\} \quad \text{ for } m \ne 2\\ &= \frac{1}{C\,(\triangle \sigma \,Q \sqrt{\pi})^2} \, \Big\{ \ln (\dfrac{a_f}{a_i}) \Big\} \qquad \quad \text{when } m = 2, \end{aligned}\] Where \(N_f\) is the number of cycles for a crack to grow from initial length \(a_i\) to a final length \(a_f\). In the equation, C and m are material properties, Q is the geometry and load factor, and \(\triangle \sigma\) is the magnitude of stress variation in one cycle.
