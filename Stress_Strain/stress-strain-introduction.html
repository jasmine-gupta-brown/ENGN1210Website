Normal and Shear Stresses
Forces can act in any direction on a surface.

\(\sigma = \dfrac{N}{A}\)
\(\tau = \dfrac{\nu}{A}\)
\(\sigma = \dfrac{F cos\theta}{A}\)
shear stress = \(\tau = \dfrac{F sin\theta}{A}\)


Example: Stresses on area defined by plane a - b ?

take a cut at a - b and draw

For c - s area a b : -
\[\begin{array}{ccc} \text{Normal Force} \hspace{0.3em} N_{a b} = N sin \theta\\ \\ \text{Shear Force} \hspace{0.3em} V_{a b} = N cos \theta\\ \\ \text{Area}\hspace{0.5em} a b = A_{a b} = \dfrac{A}{sin \theta}\\ \\ \sigma_{a b} = \dfrac{N sin \theta}{ A_{a b} } = \dfrac{N sin 2\theta}{A}\\ \\ \tau_{a b} = \dfrac{N cos\theta}{A_{a b}} = \dfrac{N sin\theta cos\theta}{A} \end{array}\]
Note : - area A in denominator is deformed area (current area).

Linear elastic relation
\[\begin{array}{ccc} \sigma = E \epsilon\\ \\ \Rightarrow \dfrac{F}{A} = E \dfrac{\Delta L}{L_o}\\ \\ \Rightarrow \dfrac{F}{\Delta L} = \dfrac{A E}{L_o}\\ \\ \text{since stiffness (of structure) is}\hspace{0.5em} k_{axial} = \dfrac{F}{\Delta L}\\ \Rightarrow \text{stiffness} \hspace{1em} k_{axial} = \dfrac{A}{L_o} E\\ \end{array}\]
where \(\dfrac{A}{L_o} \rightarrow\) geometry dependent
E \(\rightarrow\) material property dependent
Stiffness of a structure depends on both geometry and material property.


Achilles Tendon Problem

Q: what would be the max tendon elongation?
Given:

  • Person weight = 75 kg (mass)

  • Slow walking

  • Tendon length \(\sim\) 7.5 cm

  • Tendon diameter \(\sim\) 10 mm (assumed uniform cylindrical)

Force Balance : - \[F + N = R\] Moment Balance about B : - \[\begin{array}{ccc} F 4 = N 12\\ \\ \Rightarrow F = 3 N = 3 W = 3 M g\\ \end{array}\]
Maximum force case is when weight is supported only on one foot.
\[\begin{array}{ccc} \Rightarrow F = 9.81 \times 75 \times 3 = 2207.3 N\\ \\ \text{stress in tendon (axial)} \hspace{0.5em}\sigma = \dfrac{F}{A} = \dfrac{F}{\pi \dfrac{d^2}{4}} = \dfrac{2207.3 [ N ]}{ 78.5 [ mm^2] }\\ \\ \hspace{5em} = 28.1 MPa\\ \\ \text{strain} \hspace{0.5em} = \dfrac{\sigma}{E} = \dfrac{28.1 [ MPa ]}{ 1000 [ MPa ] }\\ \\ \hspace{3em} \approx 0.028 \\ \\ \hspace{5.5em} = 2.8 \% \hspace{0.3em} \text{strain} \end{array}\]
\(\therefore\) Elongation in tendon
\[\begin{array}{ccc} \Rightarrow \Delta L = L E\\ \\ \hspace{3em} = 7.5 [cm] \times 0.028\\ \\ \hspace{3em} \Rightarrow \Delta L = 0.21\text{ cm }\\ \end{array}\]
Typically walking load on achilles tendon
\(\simeq\) 3.5 to 4
\(\times\) body weight
for running \(\simeq\) 7 to 8 times body weight
Simple Composite Structures\(\rightarrow\)
e.g. consider composite consisting of tissue/cartilage with bone prosthesis with bone mechanical biomedical devices
Consider Series\(\rightarrow\)

In Y-Direction
\(\sigma, \epsilon\) are total geo. stress and strain
Materials \(\rightarrow\) properly glued
Force \(\rightarrow\) assumed to get uniformly distributed
Find \(\epsilon\) ?
\(E_{eff.} ? = \dfrac{\sigma}{\epsilon}\)

  1. Force balance where applicable : -
    Along y - direction : -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} F = F_A = F_B\\ \hspace{2.5em}\uparrow\\ \text{y - direction force in A material}\\ \\ \Rightarrow \sigma = \sigma_A = \sigma_B = \dfrac{F}{L b} \end{array}\]

  2. Geometric (deformation) compatibility : -
    Total extension in 2 direction: -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} \Delta t = \Delta t_A + \Delta t_B\\ \\ \epsilon_A = \dfrac{\Delta t_A}{t_A}; \hspace{0.5em} \epsilon_B = \dfrac{\Delta t_B}{t_B}\\ \\ \Rightarrow \epsilon = \dfrac{\Delta t}{t_A + t_B} = \dfrac{\Delta t_A + \Delta t_B}{t_A + t_B} \end{array}\]

  3. Constitutive Relation : -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} \sigma_A = E_A \epsilon_A; \hspace{0.5em}\sigma_B = E_B \epsilon_B\\ \\ \therefore \hspace{0.3em} \Delta t_A = \epsilon_A t_A = \dfrac{\sigma_A}{E_A} t_A = \dfrac{F}{E_A L b} t_A\\ \\ \text{similarly} \hspace{0.5em} \Delta t_B = \dfrac{F}{E_B L b} t_B\\ \\ \Rightarrow \epsilon = \dfrac{\Delta t_A + \Delta t_B}{t_A + t_B} \\ \hspace{1.2em}\uparrow \\ \text{strain along y in the composite}\\ \\ \Rightarrow \epsilon = \underbrace{\dfrac{F}{L b}}\hspace{0.5em} \underbrace{\dfrac{t_A / E_A + t_B / E_B}{ t_A + t_B }}\\ \hspace{3.6em} \downarrow \hspace{4.5em}\downarrow\\ \hspace{3.5em} \sigma \hspace{4.5em} E_{eff}\\ \\ \end{array}\]

In conventional send for the composite stiffness in y -direction
\[\begin{array}{lll} \Rightarrow k = \dfrac{F}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{F}{\Delta t_A + \Delta t_B}\\ \\ \Rightarrow k = \dfrac{\cancel {F}}{\dfrac{\cancel{F}}{L b} \left( \dfrac{t_A}{E_A} + \dfrac{t_B}{E_B} \right)}\\ \\ k = \dfrac{L b}{\left( \dfrac{t_A}{E_A} + \dfrac{t_B}{E_B} \right)} \end{array}\]
Consider materials glued in parallel

  1. Force balance where applicable : -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} F = F_A + F_B = \sigma_A b t_A + \sigma_B b t_B\\ \\ \text{also}\hspace{0.5em} \sigma = \dfrac{F}{b ( t_A + t_B ) } \end{array}\]

  2. Geometric (deformation) compatibility : -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} \Delta l = \Delta l_A = \Delta l_B\\ \\ \Rightarrow \epsilon_A = \epsilon_B = \epsilon = \Delta l_A / l = \Delta l_B / l = \Delta l /l \end{array}\]

  3. Constitutive Model Equations : -
    \[\begin{array}{lll} \sigma_A = E_A \epsilon_A \\ \sigma_B = E_B \epsilon_B\\ \\ \sigma = \dfrac{F}{b ( t_A + t_B )} = \dfrac{\cancel {b} (\sigma_A t_A + \sigma_B t_B ) }{\cancel {b} ( t_A + t_B )}\\ \\ \Rightarrow \sigma = \dfrac{E_A \epsilon_A t_A + E_B \epsilon_B t_B }{t_A + t_B} =\epsilon \hspace{0.3em} \underbrace{\left( \dfrac{E_A t_A + E_B t_B}{t_A + t_B} \right)}\\ \hspace{16.5em}\downarrow\\ \hspace{16em} E_{eff}\\ \end{array}\]

Elastic Energy Storage
Ex. Elastic energy storage in your tendon can contribute up to 35% of your energy need when running
Theory : - Energy stored = Work done (Elastic bodies)
(Total)

\[\begin{array}{lll} d U = F d L = d W \\ \\ \Rightarrow U = \int \limits_{L_o}^L F d L\\ \\ \hspace{2em}\because \hspace{0.5em} d \epsilon = \dfrac{ d L}{L},\\ \\ \hspace{4em} \text{and} \hspace{0.3em} F = \sigma A,\\ \\ \Rightarrow U = \int \limits_{0}^{\epsilon} \sigma A L d \epsilon\\ \\ \Rightarrow U = A L \int \limits_0^{\epsilon} \sigma d \epsilon \\ \\ \Rightarrow \boxed{ u = \dfrac{U}{A L} = \int \limits_0^{\epsilon} \sigma d \epsilon}\\ \hspace{1.7em}\uparrow\\ \text{energy stored \underline{per unit volume} (energy density)}\\ \\ \text{For linear elastic materials}\hspace{0.3em} \sigma = E \epsilon\\ \\ \Rightarrow \boxed{u = \int \limits_0^{\epsilon} E \epsilon d \epsilon = \dfrac{1}{2} E \epsilon^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} \sigma \epsilon = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2 E}} \end{array}\]
Poisson’s Ratio
Even when we are talking about simple axial problems,the geometry is 3D and strain is occurring in other directions.
Consider simple tension case

Poisson’s ration \(\upsilon\) is measure of change in lateral direction due to axially applied load.
strain \(\perp\) to 1 direction
\(\swarrow\)
\[\boxed{\upsilon \mathrel{\stackrel{\makebox[0pt]{\mbox{\normalfont\tiny def}}}{=}}\dfrac{- \Delta \omega/ \omega} {\Delta L / L}}\]
\(\nwarrow\)
strain in 1 direction
\(\upsilon \simeq 0.5\) for in-compressible materials
\(0 < \upsilon < \dfrac{1}{2}\) (conventional materials)
Hooke’s Law for Shear : -

\[\begin{array}{ccc} \boxed{\tau = G Y}\\ \\ \text{Generally for 3D consideration} \hspace{0.3em} \boxed{ \tau_{x y} = G Y_{x y}}\\ \hspace{18em}\uparrow\\ \hspace{17em}\text{change in angle (engineering shear strain)}\\ \\ Y_{x y} \approx tan Y_{x y} = \dfrac{\Delta L}{L}\\ \\ Y \leftarrow \text{small value (small strain)}\\ \\ \hspace{3em}\boxed{ \epsilon_{x y} \mathrel{\stackrel{\makebox[0pt]{\mbox{\normalfont\tiny def}}}{=}}\dfrac{Y_{x y}}{2}}\\ \uparrow\\ \text{tensoral}\\ \\ \text{Note : - }\hspace{0.5em} \boxed{ \tau_{i j} = \tau _{j i}} \end{array}\]

\[\begin{array}{ccc} \Rightarrow \text{similarly} \hspace{0.5em}\boxed{\begin{matrix} \tau_{x y} = 2 G \epsilon_{x y}\\ \tau_{y z} = 2 G \epsilon_{y z}\\ \tau_{x z} = 2 G \epsilon_{x z}\\ \end{matrix}}\\ \\ \text{ G : - Shear Modulus }\\ \text{and} \hspace{0.3em} \boxed{ G = \dfrac{E}{2 ( 1 + \upsilon )}} \end{array}\]
